Is Ho Chi Minh City Becoming Too Expensive to Live In?

If you ask me whether Ho Chi Minh City is becoming too expensive to live in, honestly, I would say yes, at least for some groups of people.

I've lived in the city for many years, so I've had the chance to see how the cost of living has changed.

And personally, I think life in Ho Chi Minh City has become noticeably more expensive.

However, I also think the situation is more complicated than simply saying, “The city is too expensive.”

The first thing people usually talk about is housing.

Personally, I think housing is probably the biggest financial pressure for many people living in Ho Chi Minh City.

If you want to live in a convenient location, rent can be quite expensive.

And if you want to buy a home, the situation can be even more difficult.

Personally, I think the gap between property prices and average incomes is a serious concern.

Many young people work hard and have a stable job.

But buying an apartment can still feel like an extremely long-term financial commitment.

And honestly, I think this is one reason why some young people delay buying a home or even decide to leave the city.

Rent is also a major issue.

If you live close to your workplace, you may have to pay more.

But if you move further away, you may save money on rent while spending more time and money commuting.

Personally, I think this creates a difficult choice.

You are almost forced to choose between your money and your time.

And honestly, neither option is perfect.

The cost of food has also changed.

Of course, you can still find relatively cheap local food in Ho Chi Minh City.

Personally, I think this is still one of the advantages of living here.

You can eat a simple meal without spending a huge amount of money.

However, if you look at your overall lifestyle, the costs can add up very quickly.

Coffee, takeaway food, transportation and occasional entertainment may not seem expensive individually.

But when you add everything together, the monthly cost can become surprisingly high.

Personally, I think many people underestimate this.

They look at one small expense and think it is affordable.

But after thirty days, the total amount can be quite significant.

Transportation is another part of the cost of living.

Many people use motorbikes, which are relatively convenient.

But fuel, maintenance and parking still cost money.

If you use taxis or ride-hailing services frequently, the expenses can become even higher.

Personally, I think commuting is a hidden cost that people don't always think about when they decide where to live.

Healthcare and education can also create financial pressure.

Of course, there are different levels of services.

But families with children may have many expenses to consider.

Personally, I think the cost of raising a family in a large city is very different from the cost of living alone.

This is one reason why I think people have very different opinions about whether Ho Chi Minh City is expensive.

For a single person with a good salary, the city may still feel manageable.

But for a family with children and a large housing loan, the financial pressure can be much greater.

Income is probably the most important part of the whole discussion.

Personally, I don't think we can simply ask whether Ho Chi Minh City is expensive.

We also need to ask, “Expensive compared with what income?”

A person earning a high salary may be able to enjoy restaurants, cafés and entertainment regularly.

Someone earning a more average income may have to carefully control their spending.

Personally, I think this is where the problem becomes serious.

The city may still be affordable for some people, but increasingly difficult for others.

Another thing I notice is the pressure to maintain a certain lifestyle.

Personally, I think social media has made this more noticeable.

People see others travelling, eating at expensive restaurants or living in attractive apartments.

And sometimes, they feel that they also need to keep up.

Honestly, I think this can create financial stress.

People may spend money not because they truly need something, but because they feel they are falling behind.

Of course, I don't think Ho Chi Minh City is expensive in every possible way.

Personally, I still think the city offers many affordable options.

You can find cheap local food.

Public transportation and motorbikes can be relatively practical.

And there are many free or low-cost activities.

For example, people can walk around public areas, visit local markets or simply spend time with friends.

So, personally, I think people can still live economically if they are willing to adjust their lifestyle.

However, I also think that lifestyle choices are not the whole answer.

Personally, I don't think it is fair to tell people, “Just spend less.”

If housing prices and basic costs continue to rise faster than people's incomes, individuals can only do so much.

This is where I think the city and the wider economy need to consider the bigger picture.

Another interesting question is whether people should leave Ho Chi Minh City if they feel it is too expensive.

Personally, I think this depends on their priorities.

The city still offers many job opportunities.

There are also more services, businesses and professional networks.

For some people, the higher cost is worth it because of the opportunities available.

But for others, especially people who can work remotely, moving to a smaller city may be a very reasonable decision.

Personally, I think people are increasingly thinking about this.

They are not only asking, “Where can I earn the most money?”

They are also asking, “Where can I live a better life?”

And honestly, I think that is a very important change.

Personally, I also think the city needs more affordable housing options.

Not everyone needs a luxury apartment.

Many people simply want a safe and reasonably comfortable place to live.

If new housing is designed mainly for people with very high incomes, a large part of the population may struggle to find suitable accommodation.

I think this is something urban planners and developers need to consider.

So, if you ask me directly whether Ho Chi Minh City is becoming too expensive, I would say that it is becoming increasingly expensive for ordinary people, especially when it comes to housing.

But I don't think the entire city is unaffordable.

Personally, I think the real problem is the growing gap between the cost of living and people's income.

The city still has many advantages.

It offers jobs, opportunities and a very active lifestyle.

But at the same time, people need to think carefully about their financial situation and what kind of life they actually want.

In my opinion, Ho Chi Minh City should not simply focus on becoming a richer and more modern city.

It also needs to remain a place where ordinary people can realistically live and build a future.

Personally, I understand why some people want stricter limits.

I've lived in Ho Chi Minh City for many years, and personally, I've noticed how quickly some areas have changed.

In the past, certain neighbourhoods may have had more low-rise buildings and open space.

Now, you may see new apartment projects appearing one after another.

And honestly, this can completely change the character of an area.

The first argument in favour of limiting high-rise apartments is probably infrastructure.

Personally, I think this is the most important issue.

A new apartment building can bring hundreds or even thousands of residents into one area.

These people need roads, water, electricity and waste management.

They may also need schools, hospitals and other public services.

So, personally, I think the city should always ask an important question before approving a large project.

“Can the existing infrastructure handle the additional population?”

If the answer is no, then I think the project needs to be reconsidered carefully.

Traffic is another major concern.

Personally, I think this is something that many residents experience directly.

Imagine a road that is already busy.

Then several large apartment projects are built along the same route.

Each project adds more residents and more vehicles.

Over time, the pressure on the road can become enormous.

And honestly, I think this is one of the reasons why some areas of Ho Chi Minh City feel increasingly congested.

Personally, I don't think the problem is simply that there are too many people.

The bigger issue is that housing development and transportation infrastructure do not always grow at the same speed.

Schools are another concern.

Personally, I think this is often overlooked when people talk about new apartment projects.

A large building may attract many families with children.

But if there are not enough schools nearby, parents may have to travel a long distance to take their children to school.

And honestly, that creates even more traffic.

The same logic applies to hospitals and other public services.

Personally, I think a city needs to plan for the people who will actually live in these buildings.

It is not enough to simply construct the apartments and expect the rest of the city to adapt later.

Another issue is the pressure on the local environment.

High-rise buildings can replace open areas and change the landscape of a neighbourhood.

Personally, I think some areas are becoming increasingly dense.

There may be less open space, less greenery and fewer places for people to relax.

Of course, I understand that Ho Chi Minh City needs to develop.

The population is large, and people need somewhere to live.

Personally, I don't think we can realistically say, “Let's stop building apartments.”

If the city completely stops new housing development, property prices could become even higher.

Rent could also increase.

And honestly, ordinary people may suffer the most.

So, personally, I think this is why the debate is so complicated.

We need more housing, but we also need better planning.

High-rise apartments also have some clear advantages.

Personally, I think they can be a more efficient way to use limited land.

Instead of spreading homes across a huge area, many people can live in one building.

This can potentially reduce urban expansion.

And if apartment buildings are located near public transportation, they may even encourage people to use cars and motorbikes less.

Personally, I think high-rise living is not automatically a bad thing.

The problem is how and where these buildings are developed.

For example, a large apartment complex near a metro station could make a lot of sense.

Residents could use public transportation to go to work.

There may also be shops and services within walking distance.

But personally, I think building several large projects in an area with poor transportation is a very different situation.

The density may simply become too high.

Another concern is the quality of life for existing residents.

Personally, I think people sometimes feel that their neighbourhood changes too quickly.

A quiet area can suddenly become much busier.

There may be more traffic, more noise and more construction.

And honestly, local residents may feel that they have no control over the process.

I think this is an important social issue.

Urban development should not only consider investors and future residents.

It should also consider the people who already live there.

At the same time, I understand the perspective of property developers.

Personally, I think they are responding to real demand.

People want housing.

Investors want to develop land.

And the city needs economic growth.

So, I don't think it is fair to simply blame developers for every urban problem.

Personally, I think the bigger issue is the planning system.

If a project is approved, the city should already have a clear plan for transportation and public services.

The city should not wait until the roads become completely congested before trying to solve the problem.

Personally, I think the answer is probably not a simple limit on the number of high-rise apartments.

Instead, I would support stricter conditions.

For example, large apartment projects should be approved only if the surrounding infrastructure can support them.

There should also be a clear connection between housing development and public transportation.

Personally, I think this is much more logical than simply saying, “No more high-rise buildings.”

Another possible solution is to develop different parts of the city more evenly.

At the moment, some areas are becoming extremely crowded while other areas have much more space.

Personally, I think the city should encourage more balanced development.

People should not be forced to concentrate in only a few areas.

But of course, this requires jobs and services to be developed in those areas as well.

You cannot simply build apartments far away and expect people to live there happily if they still have to travel two hours to work every day.

Personally, I think this is a very important point.

Housing, jobs and transportation need to be planned together.

So, if you ask me whether Ho Chi Minh City should limit the number of new high-rise apartments, my answer would be, “Not necessarily, but the city should be much more careful about where and how they are built.”

Personally, I think high-rise apartments are part of the future of a large city.

They can provide housing and make better use of limited land.

But if they are built without proper planning, they can create serious problems for the entire neighbourhood.

In my opinion, the real issue is not simply the number of buildings.

It is the relationship between population density and urban infrastructure.

And personally, I think Ho Chi Minh City needs to focus more on that relationship.

The city should develop, but it should develop in a way that makes life better for residents rather than simply making the skyline more impressive.
