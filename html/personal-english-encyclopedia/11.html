But personally, I think we need to understand the underlying problem.

Another thing I find interesting is the difference between different parts of the city.

Some sidewalks are relatively wide and well-maintained.

Other sidewalks are extremely narrow.

In some areas, there may be trees, utility poles or other obstacles.

Personally, I think the quality of sidewalks can vary significantly.

And this makes it difficult to create one simple rule for the entire city.

For example, a very wide sidewalk may be able to accommodate both pedestrians and a limited amount of commercial activity.

But a narrow sidewalk may barely be large enough for people to walk.

Personally, I think the city needs to consider the actual physical conditions of each area.

One thing I personally dislike is when pedestrians have to constantly change direction.

You may walk ten metres and then suddenly face an obstacle.

You move to the left.

Then you move to the right.

And sometimes you even have to walk on the road.

Honestly, I think this makes walking in the city quite tiring.

And it also sends a message that pedestrians are not the priority.

Personally, I think that is something the city should take more seriously.

On the other hand, I also think sidewalks are an important part of Ho Chi Minh City's culture.

If you remove all activity from the sidewalks, the city may become much more organized.

But personally, I think it could also lose some of its personality.

Street food, small cafés and people sitting outside are part of the city's atmosphere.

When I think about Ho Chi Minh City, I don't imagine completely empty sidewalks.

I imagine people walking, eating and interacting with each other.

So, personally, I don't think the solution is to make sidewalks completely empty.

The question is how to manage them better.

For example, certain areas could be designed for specific purposes.

Some sections could focus mainly on pedestrians.

Other areas might allow limited commercial activity under clear conditions.

Personally, I think this would be more realistic than trying to apply exactly the same approach everywhere.

Enforcement is also important.

Honestly, I think one of the biggest problems is inconsistency.

Sometimes rules are strictly enforced.

At other times, people seem to use the sidewalk in whatever way they want.

Personally, I think businesses and residents need clear and consistent rules.

If the rules change depending on the location or the situation, people may become confused.

And eventually, they may simply ignore the rules.

I also think the public needs to understand that sidewalks are shared spaces.

A business cannot treat the entire sidewalk as private property.

At the same time, pedestrians also need to behave responsibly.

For example, people should not deliberately block the walking area or create unnecessary problems.

Personally, I think the key word is **coexistence**.

Different groups need to use the city together.

But coexistence requires some boundaries.

If everyone simply takes as much space as they want, the people with the least power often suffer the most.

And in this case, that is frequently pedestrians.

Personally, I think the sidewalk issue also reflects a larger problem in Ho Chi Minh City.

The city has developed extremely quickly.

The number of people, vehicles and businesses has increased significantly.

But urban infrastructure and public space have not always developed at the same speed.

So, many people are constantly competing for limited space.

And honestly, I think the sidewalk problem is one visible result of that situation.

If you ask me directly whether sidewalks should be public space or parking space, I would say they should primarily be public space.

People need to be able to walk safely.

However, I don't think the city should completely ignore the needs of businesses and motorbike users.

Personally, I think the best solution is better planning, clearer rules and more realistic enforcement.

The city needs to provide more parking options and design public spaces more carefully.

At the same time, businesses need to understand that the sidewalk does not belong to them.

So, if I had to summarize my opinion, I would say that sidewalks in Ho Chi Minh City are currently being asked to do too many things.

They are expected to be walking paths, parking areas, commercial spaces and social spaces at the same time.

Personally, I think the challenge is to decide what should come first.

And in my opinion, pedestrians should have priority.

But at the same time, the city needs to find a practical way for businesses and local communities to continue using public space without completely blocking the people who are simply trying to walk.
