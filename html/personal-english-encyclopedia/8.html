At The Airport

When people hear the word “airport”, they probably think about airplanes or traveling abroad.

But for me, as someone who's lived in Ho Chi Minh City for many years, an airport is actually a place I'm quite familiar with.

I've been to Tan Son Nhat Airport quite a few times, and I think it's a really interesting place to observe people.

To me, it's not just somewhere you go to catch a flight.

It's almost like a gateway to the city, and in some ways, it feels like a completely different world from the rest of Ho Chi Minh City.

Outside the airport, you have the usual traffic, motorbikes and noise that the city is famous for.

But once you get inside the terminal, the atmosphere changes quite a bit.

The space feels much more organized and international.

You see high ceilings, bright lights, large electronic departure screens and signs pointing you in different directions.

And honestly, I think the people are probably the most interesting part of any airport.

You can see all kinds of passengers there.

Some people are business travelers, and they walk really quickly with a laptop bag in one hand and their phone in the other.

Then you have tourists carrying suitcases, families traveling with young children, and elderly people who sometimes look a little confused about where they should go.

I also find it interesting to look at people's expressions.

Some passengers look completely relaxed, while others are clearly in a hurry.

They keep checking their phones, looking at the departure board and checking their passports or boarding passes again and again.

You can usually tell when someone is nervous about flying, especially if it's their first time.

They tend to look around a lot and pay very close attention to every announcement.

And of course, airports are full of emotional moments as well.

One thing I often notice is people saying goodbye to each other.

Sometimes you see parents hugging their children before they go through the security area.

Sometimes a couple is trying to enjoy the last few minutes together before one of them leaves for another country.

I think those moments can be quite emotional because, after the security checkpoint, you can't simply walk back and continue the conversation.

You really have to say goodbye.

But on the other hand, there are also lots of happy reunions.

At the arrival area, you might see people waiting with flowers, balloons or just huge smiles on their faces.

They may be waiting for a family member or someone they haven't seen for months.

So, in a strange way, I think airports are places full of both goodbyes and reunions.

From a passenger's point of view, the airport also has a very clear process.

Usually, you start by checking in and dropping off your luggage if you have any large bags.

Some people still prefer going to a counter, while others use self-service kiosks if they're available.

After that, you go through the security checkpoint.

Personally, I think this is the point where most people suddenly become much more serious.

Everyone starts checking their belongings and making sure they haven't forgotten anything.

For international flights, there are usually more procedures to go through, such as immigration.

Then, once you get to the airside area, you find your boarding gate and basically start waiting.

And waiting is actually a big part of the airport experience.

People sit around, charge their phones, grab a coffee or something to eat, and try to kill time.

Some people just stare at the departure screen every few minutes.

Others walk around the terminal because they don't want to sit in the same place for too long.

Personally, I quite like observing people while I'm waiting.

You can almost make up stories about them just by looking at their luggage and their behavior.

The airport is also a pretty important commercial space.

There are restaurants, coffee shops, convenience stores and all kinds of services.

Some airports also have duty-free shops and lounges for business travelers.

I think food and coffee are especially important because passengers often arrive early and may have a couple of hours to kill before boarding.

Something else I find quite interesting is the number of people working behind the scenes.

Most passengers only notice the check-in staff or security officers, but an airport actually depends on a huge number of workers.

There are ground staff, baggage handlers, cleaners, maintenance workers, pilots and cabin crew.

All of them have to do their jobs at roughly the right time.

When I think about it, an airport is almost like a small city.

Thousands of people are moving around, different teams are working at the same time, and everything has to be coordinated quite carefully.

Of course, airports can also be stressful.

There can be long queues, flight delays or sudden gate changes.

And in Ho Chi Minh City, I think getting to the airport can sometimes be stressful enough by itself because of the traffic.

That's why I personally prefer leaving home early.

I'd much rather arrive early and have a coffee than arrive at the last minute and have to run through the terminal.

But what I like most about airports is probably the feeling that something is about to happen.

When I'm sitting near a boarding gate and I hear an announcement for my flight, I get this feeling that I'm about to leave my normal routine behind for a while.

So, technically, an airport is just a place where people catch flights.

But honestly, I think it's much more than that.

It's a place full of movement, technology, business and emotion.

And because I've lived in Ho Chi Minh City for many years, Tan Son Nhat Airport is a place I'm quite familiar with.

I'm definitely not an aviation expert, but I do enjoy watching the people there and thinking about how every passenger is probably starting or ending a completely different story.

Bui Vien Street

If someone asked me where the busiest nightlife in Ho Chi Minh City is, I would probably say Bui Vien Street without thinking too much.

I've lived in Ho Chi Minh City for many years, and I've been to Bui Vien quite a few times.

So, I wouldn't say I'm a party person, but I know the street pretty well.

And honestly, Bui Vien is one of those places that is really difficult to explain to someone who has never been there.

You have to actually walk through it at night to understand the atmosphere.

During the daytime, Bui Vien is just a fairly ordinary street in District 1.

There are restaurants, small hotels, convenience stores and various businesses.

But once the sun goes down, especially at the weekend, the whole street seems to transform.

It's almost like someone turns a switch on.

The lights become much brighter, music starts coming from different directions, and more and more people begin filling the street.

Before you know it, the place is packed.

The first thing that really hits you is the noise.

You can hear music from several bars at the same time.

One place might be playing EDM, another might have live music, and somewhere else you can hear people cheering or shouting.

On top of that, there are conversations happening in different languages all around you.

So, if you stand in the middle of Bui Vien, you don't just hear one sound.

You hear a mixture of music, laughter, traffic, people talking and sometimes even street performers.

It's loud, chaotic and a little overwhelming.

But I think that's exactly what attracts people to the street.

The people are probably the most interesting part of Bui Vien.

You see a huge mix of nationalities and different types of people.

There are young backpackers carrying small backpacks and looking for somewhere to have a drink.

There are foreign tourists who seem to be enjoying their first real night in Ho Chi Minh City.

You also see expats, local Vietnamese people, groups of friends and couples.

Some people are clearly there to party.

You can tell because they are already dressed for a night out and seem to be moving from one bar to another.

Other people are much more relaxed.

They just sit on small plastic chairs, have a drink and watch the crowd go by.

Personally, I think Bui Vien is a very good place for people-watching.

You can literally stand on one side of the street and watch dozens of little stories happening at the same time.

For example, you might see a group of foreign tourists trying to decide which bar to go to.

A few metres away, a local group of friends is taking photos.

Then you see someone walking around alone, probably looking for a place to sit down and meet new people.

And because there are so many international visitors, English is heard everywhere.

The interesting thing is that Bui Vien has a very international atmosphere, but it still feels very Vietnamese at the same time.

A lot of the bars are open to the street.

Instead of being completely separated from the outside world, they almost become part of the street itself.

You can sit on a small chair, have a cold beer and watch people walking past you.

Some bars have live bands, some play loud electronic music, and others are more focused on casual drinking.

Personally, I think the open-front design of many bars is a big part of the Bui Vien experience.

You don't necessarily have to commit to one place for the whole night.

You can just walk around, look at the atmosphere and decide where you want to stop.

That's why bar-hopping is quite common there.

And, of course, the street is not only about drinking.

There are plenty of restaurants and small food places around the area.

After spending a few hours walking around or having drinks, many people suddenly become hungry.

You can find all kinds of food, from Vietnamese dishes to food aimed more at international tourists.

Some places are quite casual, while others are more like proper restaurants.

Personally, I think late-night food is an important part of the whole experience.

A night out doesn't always end when the music stops.

Sometimes people are hungry at midnight, or even later, and they just want something hot and easy to eat.

Another thing you often see in Bui Vien is street entertainment.

There may be live music, performers or people simply trying to create a fun atmosphere.

The exact activity can change from one night to another, but the street always feels like something is happening.

And I think this is one reason why Bui Vien feels different from a normal nightlife area.

You are not just sitting inside a club.

A lot of the action is happening right in front of you, on the street.

Of course, Bui Vien also has a lot of people working there.

There are bartenders, waiters, restaurant staff, hotel workers, street food vendors and people responsible for cleaning and maintaining the area.

You also see people standing outside bars and trying to attract customers.

They may invite passers-by to take a look at their bar or sit down for a drink.

If you know the street well, you start to understand how the whole place works.

Some people are there to serve customers, some are there to make money, and some are simply there to enjoy the nightlife.

And because the street is so busy, the crowd itself is constantly changing.

At around seven or eight in the evening, the atmosphere is usually quite lively but still manageable.

Later at night, the crowd becomes much denser and the energy gets stronger.

People are louder, the bars are busier and it becomes much harder to walk through the street without bumping into someone.

Personally, I think the most interesting thing about Bui Vien is the contrast.

On one hand, it looks extremely modern and international.

You see foreign tourists, English signs, electronic music and people from all over the world.

But on the other hand, you still have very typical Vietnamese things happening around you.

You see small plastic chairs, street food, casual conversations and that very familiar kind of urban chaos that you can find in Ho Chi Minh City.

It's a strange combination, but somehow it works.

Of course, I wouldn't say Bui Vien is perfect.

It can be extremely noisy, especially for people living or staying nearby.

The street can also feel overcrowded, and some people may find the atmosphere too aggressive or too intense.

Personally, I don't think it's the kind of place I would want to visit every single weekend.

Sometimes, after a long working week, I would rather have a quiet coffee or stay at home.

But if a friend from another city or another country came to Ho Chi Minh City and asked me to show them the city's nightlife, I would probably take them to Bui Vien at least once.

Not because I think it's the most beautiful place in the city.

Actually, it's quite the opposite.

Bui Vien can be messy, loud and chaotic.

But that's also why it feels so alive.

To me, Bui Vien is like a small snapshot of Ho Chi Minh City at night.

It's energetic, crowded, a little unpredictable and full of different kinds of people.

And honestly, I think the best way to describe it is this: Bui Vien is not a place you simply visit.

It's a place you experience.

You walk through it, listen to the noise, watch the crowd and let the atmosphere carry you along.

That's probably why, even after all these years, Bui Vien is still one of the most recognizable nightlife spots in Ho Chi Minh City.

A Typical Morning

If you ask me what a typical morning in Ho Chi Minh City looks like, I would probably say it's busy, noisy and surprisingly energetic.

I've lived in Ho Chi Minh City for many years, so I'm quite used to the way the city wakes up.

And honestly, I think the morning is one of the best times to understand how the city really works.

The day usually starts quite early for a lot of people.

When I leave my apartment in the morning, I can already see people heading to work and children being taken to school.

Some people are walking quickly, while others are already on their motorbikes.

You can almost feel that the whole city is slowly switching on.

The first thing I notice is the traffic.

There are motorbikes everywhere, especially on the main roads.

People are trying to get to work, students are going to school and delivery drivers are starting their working day.

At that time of the morning, everyone seems to have somewhere to be.

Personally, I think motorbikes are still a huge part of the morning atmosphere in Ho Chi Minh City.

You see people wearing office clothes, students in uniforms and workers carrying all kinds of things on their motorbikes.

Some people are wearing helmets and looking completely awake, while others look like they are still trying to wake up.

And then you have the breakfast scene.

I think breakfast in Ho Chi Minh City is a really interesting thing to observe.

A lot of people don't eat at home.

Instead, they stop somewhere on the way to work.

You can see small food stalls opening early in the morning.

Some sell banh mi, some serve noodles, and others sell sticky rice or different kinds of Vietnamese breakfast.

There are usually small plastic chairs and tables on the pavement, and people sit down for a quick meal before continuing their day.

I actually like this part of the morning because it feels very local and very Vietnamese.

People don't necessarily spend an hour having breakfast.

They sit down, eat fairly quickly, have a short conversation and then get back on their motorbike.

Coffee is another very important part of the morning.

You can see coffee shops opening early, and many people stop to buy a cup of coffee before going to work.

Some people grab a takeaway coffee, while others sit down and chat with their friends.

Personally, I think Vietnamese people have a very strong coffee culture.

For many people, coffee is not just a drink.

It's almost part of their daily routine.

Another thing I notice is how different people behave in the morning.

Some people look extremely focused.

They are probably already thinking about work and their schedule for the day.

Other people seem much more relaxed.

They might be sitting at a roadside coffee shop and chatting with a friend.

And then, of course, there are people who are clearly in a hurry.

You can usually recognize them immediately.

They keep checking the time, looking at their phones and trying to move through the traffic as quickly as possible.

I think the morning also shows the different sides of Ho Chi Minh City.

In some areas, especially near major roads, the city feels extremely crowded and fast-paced.

But if you go into a smaller street, the atmosphere can be completely different.

You might see older people exercising, local residents buying breakfast or small shops slowly opening their doors.

So, even though the city is a huge urban area, there are still many quiet little moments in the morning.

The weather also has a big effect on the morning atmosphere.

If it's sunny, people seem to move around normally.

But if it suddenly starts raining, everything can become much more complicated.

People stop to put on raincoats, traffic slows down and some roads become crowded almost immediately.

And in Ho Chi Minh City, the weather can change quite quickly, so people often have to be prepared.

For me, one of the most interesting things about a typical morning is the feeling of movement.

Everyone is going somewhere.

People are going to work, taking their children to school, opening their businesses or delivering food.

Even though most people are doing completely different things, they are all part of the same morning rhythm.

I've experienced this for many years, so I don't always notice it consciously anymore.

But whenever I stop and think about it, I realize that a normal morning in Ho Chi Minh City is actually quite fascinating.

It's not necessarily beautiful in a traditional sense.

It's noisy, crowded and sometimes stressful.

But it's very alive.

And I think that's probably the best way to describe a typical morning in Ho Chi Minh City.

The city wakes up early, millions of people start moving around, and by the time the morning is over, the whole city is already fully active.

During Rush Hour

If you want to understand the more stressful side of Ho Chi Minh City, I think you should experience the city during rush hour.

I've lived here for many years, and I travel through the city quite regularly, so rush hour is something I'm very familiar with.

And honestly, I think the best way to describe it is controlled chaos.

At first, everything may look quite normal.

There are motorbikes, cars and buses moving along the roads.

But as more and more people start heading to work or going home, the traffic gradually becomes heavier.

Then, at some point, you realize that the road is completely packed.

The morning rush hour usually happens when people are going to work and students are going to school.

In the afternoon, the situation is slightly different because people are heading home.

Personally, I feel that the evening rush hour can sometimes be even more exhausting.

After a full day at work, people are already tired.

They just want to get home, have dinner and relax.

But instead, they may have to spend a long time sitting in traffic.

One of the most obvious things about rush hour in Ho Chi Minh City is the number of motorbikes.

There are so many of them that sometimes the whole road looks like a moving sea of motorbikes.

People move between cars, change lanes and try to find the fastest route.

Some riders are very patient and simply wait in line.

But others are clearly in a hurry.

They try to squeeze through small gaps, move to the side of the road or take a different route to avoid a traffic jam.

And to be honest, people's behavior becomes quite interesting during rush hour.

Some drivers are extremely impatient.

They keep looking for a way to move forward, even when there is almost no space.

Others seem to accept the situation and just wait.

Personally, I think you can learn quite a lot about people's personalities by watching them in traffic.

Of course, not everyone drives perfectly.

You sometimes see people suddenly changing lanes without giving a clear signal.

Some motorbike riders move between lanes quite aggressively.

And when the traffic is extremely heavy, even a small mistake can create a bigger problem.

That's why traffic jams can sometimes become worse very quickly.

Another interesting thing is that the traffic situation can change from one road to another.

You may be completely stuck on one street, but then you turn into a smaller road and suddenly everything is moving normally again.

People who have lived in Ho Chi Minh City for a long time usually have their own favorite routes.

They know which roads are usually busy, which streets are easier to travel through and which areas they should avoid at certain times.

Personally, I also try to plan my journey carefully.

I prefer leaving home early rather than taking the risk of getting stuck somewhere.

For me, arriving early is much less stressful than constantly checking the time and worrying about being late.

And traffic is not only about vehicles.

You also have pedestrians, street vendors, delivery drivers and people trying to cross the road.

Everyone is trying to use the same limited space.

This is probably one of the main reasons why rush hour feels so chaotic.

The situation can become even worse when it rains.

When it starts raining, many motorbike riders stop to put on raincoats.

Some people pull over, while others keep driving.

Traffic slows down, visibility becomes worse and certain roads may start to flood.

So, a journey that normally takes a reasonable amount of time can suddenly become much longer.

Personally, I think rush hour has a psychological effect on people as well.

When you have been stuck in traffic for a long time, you become more impatient and tired.

You start checking your phone, looking at the time and wondering how much longer you have to wait.

Some people listen to music or podcasts to make the journey feel less boring.

I actually think that is one reason why so many people in Ho Chi Minh City spend a lot of time listening to something while commuting.

After living here for many years, I've become quite used to the traffic.

I still don't like being stuck in a traffic jam, obviously.

But I think I've learned to accept that traffic is simply part of life in a big city like Ho Chi Minh City.

At the same time, I don't think we should just accept every traffic problem as normal.

The city is growing, more buildings are being developed and more people are traveling every day.

So, in my opinion, better public transport and better urban planning are becoming increasingly important.

Overall, rush hour is probably one of the most frustrating parts of living in Ho Chi Minh City.

But at the same time, it also shows how active and energetic the city is.

Millions of people are moving around, going to work, studying and trying to build their lives.

So, when I look at a huge traffic jam, I don't only see vehicles.

I also see the daily routine of an entire city.

And honestly, that's probably why rush hour is such a big part of the Ho Chi Minh City experience.

Ho Thi Ky Flower Market

If someone asked me to show them a different side of Ho Chi Minh City at night, I probably wouldn't take them to Bui Vien first.

I might take them to Ho Thi Ky Flower Market.

I've been to the area quite a few times, and honestly, I think it's one of the most interesting places in the city to experience at night.

It's not flashy or glamorous.

In fact, parts of it can look quite messy and crowded.

But that's exactly what makes it interesting.

The first thing you notice when you get there is the atmosphere.

Ho Thi Ky doesn't feel like a normal shopping area.

It feels much more like a working market.

There are people moving around, small vehicles coming and going, shop owners arranging their products and customers trying to find their way through the narrow spaces.

And then, of course, there are the flowers.

You see flowers almost everywhere.

There are huge bundles of flowers, small flower shops and different kinds of colors competing for your attention.

Some flowers are arranged quite neatly, while others are still in large bundles waiting to be sorted or delivered.

Personally, I think the colors are probably the most visually impressive part of the market.

You can see red, yellow, pink, white and purple flowers almost everywhere.

And when you walk through the market at night, the flowers stand out even more because the surrounding streets can be quite dark and crowded.

But for me, the smell is just as memorable as the colors.

When you walk around a flower market, you naturally expect the place to smell fresh.

And at Ho Thi Ky, you can definitely feel that atmosphere.

Of course, the smell is not always just flowers.

The market area is also well known for its food scene, so as you walk around, you may suddenly smell grilled food, seafood or different kinds of street snacks.

That's one of the things I find interesting about Ho Thi Ky.

You can be looking at flowers one minute and then suddenly start thinking about food.

The people are another major part of the experience.

You see flower sellers, delivery workers, local residents and people who have simply come to buy flowers.

Some sellers look extremely busy.

They are sorting flowers, carrying bundles or talking to customers.

Other people seem to know exactly what they want.

They walk into a shop, choose a particular type of flower and leave quite quickly.

And then you have ordinary visitors like me, who sometimes just walk around and look at everything.

Personally, I think you can tell that the market is a real working place.

It's not just a tourist attraction designed to look interesting.

People are actually there to do business.

Flowers are being bought, sold, sorted and moved around.

So, even when you are just visiting, you can still feel the commercial side of the market.

And I think that's quite different from many modern shopping malls.

At a shopping mall, everything is usually clean, organized and carefully designed.

At Ho Thi Ky, things feel much more spontaneous.

There are narrow paths, small shops and people working in quite limited spaces.

You sometimes have to step aside to let someone pass.

But honestly, I think that kind of chaos is part of the charm.

Another thing I like about the market is the contrast between the flowers and the surrounding environment.

Flowers are normally associated with beauty and special occasions.

People buy them for weddings, birthdays, festivals or simply to decorate their homes.

But at the market, you see the practical side of the flower business.

People are carrying them, counting them and trying to sell them.

So, you are seeing flowers as a business rather than just something beautiful.

I think the food area adds another layer to the whole experience.

There are many small food stalls and casual places to eat around the market.

People sit on small plastic chairs, order a few dishes and eat in a very relaxed way.

You may see students, young people, local residents and groups of friends.

Some people probably come specifically for the food, while others discover the food area after visiting the flower market.

Personally, I think this combination of flowers and street food is quite unusual.

You don't often see such a strong contrast in the same place.

The market also has a different rhythm depending on the time.

Earlier in the evening, you may see more people coming for food or casually walking around.

Later at night, the atmosphere can become more focused on the actual flower business.

You start to notice more activity involving flower deliveries and people working.

And if you are there very late, you really get the feeling that the market has its own schedule, which is quite different from the rest of the city.

Most people think of Ho Chi Minh City as a city that never really stops.

But at Ho Thi Ky, you can see a different kind of activity.

It's not people partying.

It's people working.

They are moving products, serving customers and preparing things for the next part of the day.

Personally, I think this is what makes the place so interesting.

When I walk around Ho Thi Ky, I don't feel like I'm simply visiting a market.

I feel like I'm getting a small glimpse into the city's working life.

You can see the people behind the products that eventually end up in flower shops, events and people's homes.

And honestly, I think that is something tourists may not always notice.

They might just see a colorful flower market and take a few photos.

But if you spend a little more time there, you start to notice the people, the movement and the business happening around you.

Of course, Ho Thi Ky is not the kind of place where you can expect everything to be perfectly comfortable.

It can be crowded, noisy and sometimes a little difficult to walk around.

But personally, I don't think that's a problem.

If I wanted a quiet and polished experience, I could go to a modern shopping mall.

I go to Ho Thi Ky because I want to see something more authentic and more connected to everyday life in Ho Chi Minh City.

So, if I had to describe Ho Thi Ky in a few words, I would say it's colorful, busy and surprisingly full of life.

It's a place where flowers, food and ordinary working people all come together.

And for me, that's what makes it one of the most interesting places to experience Ho Chi Minh City at night.
