My Japan Work Experience

When people ask me about the difference between working for a Japanese company and working for other companies, I think there are several things that stand out, especially in terms of work culture, communication, discipline and the way people plan their work.

Of course, I don't think I can speak for every Japanese company because every company is different.

My experience is mainly based on working for a Japanese company in Vietnam as a web developer.

However, compared with some other working environments I know about, I have noticed several differences.

The first thing that comes to mind is the work culture.

I think Japanese companies generally place quite a strong emphasis on discipline and responsibility.

For example, people tend to take their work quite seriously.

If you are assigned a task, you are generally expected to take responsibility for it and keep track of its progress.

Personally, I think this creates a relatively organised working environment.

People don't just do something randomly and then forget about it.

There is usually a certain process that people are expected to follow.

Another thing I notice is the importance of planning.

In my experience, Japanese teams often spend quite a lot of time discussing and planning before actually starting a task.

For example, we may need to understand the requirements, discuss possible solutions and estimate the amount of work involved.

Sometimes, I feel that the planning process can be quite detailed.

To be honest, there are moments when I just want to start coding immediately.

However, I also understand the reason behind this approach.

If you spend more time thinking about a task at the beginning, you may be able to avoid bigger problems later.

I think this is quite different from some working environments where people may start working very quickly and adjust their plans later.

Personally, I think both approaches have advantages and disadvantages.

Starting quickly can be useful when you need to move fast, but careful planning can help reduce misunderstandings and unnecessary work.

Communication is another interesting difference.

Since I work for a Japanese company, communication between the Vietnamese team and Japanese clients is obviously quite important.

In my experience, communication tends to be relatively polite and careful.

People usually try to explain things clearly and avoid being too direct in a rude way.

I think this is partly related to Japanese business culture.

There is quite a strong emphasis on politeness and respect.

At the same time, communication can sometimes be a little indirect.

For example, people may not always say directly that they dislike an idea.

Instead, they may express their concerns in a more subtle way.

For me, this means that sometimes I have to pay attention not only to the exact words people use but also to the context and the overall meaning.

Of course, communication can also be affected by the language barrier.

Even if everyone understands English reasonably well, small misunderstandings can still happen.

For example, a requirement may sound clear at first, but different people may have slightly different interpretations of it.

That's why I think asking questions and confirming important details is extremely important when working with an international team.

Another difference I have noticed is discipline and punctuality.

In general, people tend to respect schedules and deadlines quite seriously.

Meetings usually have a clear purpose, and people are expected to be prepared.

If a task is estimated to take a certain amount of time, the team usually pays attention to whether the work is progressing as expected.

Personally, I think this can be quite positive because it helps everyone understand the current situation.

However, it can also create some pressure.

If something unexpected happens, you need to communicate it clearly rather than simply staying silent.

For example, if I realise that a task is more complicated than I originally expected, I should report the situation and explain the reason.

I think this is an important working habit.

In some companies, people may try to solve a problem quietly until the last minute.

But if you do that, the problem may become much more serious.

I also think Japanese companies tend to pay a lot of attention to processes and documentation.

In software development, we may need to follow certain procedures when working on tasks, testing features or deploying changes.

Personally, I sometimes feel that there is a lot of paperwork or documentation involved.

However, I can also see the benefits.

When a project has a clear process and enough documentation, it becomes easier for new people to understand the system.

This is especially important for large projects.

You can't rely on one person remembering everything in their head.

Another thing I find interesting is the attitude towards continuous improvement.

I think Japanese work culture is often associated with the idea of improving things gradually rather than trying to completely change everything overnight.

In my work, I sometimes notice that people are interested in making small improvements to existing processes or systems.

For example, if a process is slightly inefficient, the team may discuss how to improve it step by step.

Personally, I like this way of thinking.

In software development, you often can't rewrite an entire system from scratch.

Small improvements can actually make a significant difference over time.

However, I also think there can be some disadvantages to this culture.

Because Japanese companies often have quite detailed processes and a strong focus on planning, decision-making can sometimes feel slower.

There may be several discussions or approval steps before a decision is finalised.

Compared with a smaller company where the manager can simply make a quick decision, this can feel a little frustrating.

Personally, I think this is probably one of the biggest differences I've noticed.

On the positive side, decisions may be more carefully considered.

But on the negative side, it can take more time to move forward.

I also think the working environment can depend a lot on the specific company and team.

I don't want to say that Japanese companies are always more disciplined or that other companies are disorganised.

There are definitely well-managed companies from many different countries.

For me, the biggest difference is probably the emphasis on structure, planning and process.

Working for a Japanese company has taught me to be more careful with my work.

I pay more attention to requirements, deadlines and communication.

I think these are useful skills, especially in software development.

At the same time, I have also learned that no working culture is perfect.

Sometimes, I wish decisions could be made more quickly.

Other times, I appreciate having a clear process and knowing exactly what is expected of me.

Overall, I would say working for a Japanese company is quite different from what I imagined when I first started my career.

The biggest differences, in my opinion, are the strong focus on discipline, detailed planning, careful communication and structured processes.

Personally, I think this working environment suits me reasonably well.

I still have difficult days and sometimes feel frustrated by the amount of planning or communication involved.

But overall, I think working in this environment has helped me become more organised and responsible as a developer.

So, if I had to summarise the difference in one sentence, I would say that working for a Japanese company often feels more structured and process-oriented, while other companies may sometimes offer a faster and more flexible working style.

Of course, it really depends on the specific company, but that's the main difference I've experienced in my own career.

Ho Chi Minh City Challenges

Although I really enjoy living in Ho Chi Minh City, I don't think the city is perfect.

I've lived here for many years, so I've had the chance to experience both the advantages and the problems of urban life.

If I had to talk about the main issues that Ho Chi Minh City needs to improve, I would probably mention traffic infrastructure, traffic congestion, housing prices, pollution and the way public spaces are managed.

The first and probably the most obvious problem is transportation infrastructure.

Ho Chi Minh City is a very large and crowded city, but the infrastructure has not always developed as quickly as the population.

Every day, thousands of people travel from the suburbs to the central districts for work or school.

As a result, many roads become extremely crowded during rush hour.

Personally, I experience this quite regularly because I travel to work by motorbike.

In the morning, the roads can become very busy, and the situation is usually even worse in the late afternoon when people are going home.

The problem is that simply building wider roads is not always enough.

In many areas, the number of people and vehicles has increased much faster than the infrastructure.

For example, there are some areas where many new apartment complexes have been built along the same road.

Each apartment complex may contain thousands of residents.

However, the roads, intersections and public facilities around them may not have been upgraded quickly enough.

So, you suddenly have a huge number of people living in one area, but the surrounding infrastructure is still almost the same as before.

In my opinion, the city needs to plan new residential developments more carefully.

Before allowing too many apartment projects to be built in one area, authorities should consider whether the roads, schools, hospitals and public transportation can actually support the additional population.

Another major problem is traffic congestion during rush hour.

I think traffic jams are one of the biggest sources of stress for people living in Ho Chi Minh City.

Sometimes, a journey that normally takes forty minutes can take much longer because of a serious traffic jam.

Personally, I think the city needs to develop public transportation much more seriously.

Buses are available, but they are still affected by traffic, and some routes are not very convenient for everyone.

The metro is a very promising solution, but the network still needs to be expanded.

In the future, I think the city should connect different forms of transportation more effectively.

For example, people could travel by motorbike or bus to a metro station, take the metro and then use another form of transport to reach their final destination.

If public transportation becomes more convenient and reliable, I think many people would be willing to reduce their use of private vehicles.

Another issue that concerns me is the cost of housing.

Property prices in Ho Chi Minh City have become extremely high compared with the income of many ordinary people.

For young people, especially those who are just starting their careers, buying an apartment can feel almost impossible.

Even if someone has a stable job, they may still need to take out a very large mortgage and spend decades paying it back.

Personally, I think this is a serious social issue.

A city needs to be affordable enough for ordinary workers to live in.

If housing prices become too high, people may be forced to live further and further away from the city centre.

Then they have to spend more time commuting, which creates even more traffic problems.

I think the city needs more affordable housing projects and better policies to support people with average incomes.

Another problem I notice is the declining birth rate.

I think this is partly related to the cost of living.

Young couples may want to have children, but they also have to think about housing, education, healthcare and daily expenses.

If people feel that raising a child is financially overwhelming, they may decide to have fewer children or postpone having children altogether.

In my opinion, the city needs to create a more supportive environment for young families.

This could include more affordable housing, better childcare facilities and policies that help parents balance work and family life.

Air pollution is another problem that Ho Chi Minh City needs to take more seriously.

Because there are so many vehicles on the roads, the air quality can be quite poor in certain areas, especially near major roads.

Sometimes, when I'm riding my motorbike, I can clearly feel the difference between a quiet road and a busy main road.

I think reducing air pollution requires a combination of solutions.

The city needs to improve public transportation, encourage cleaner vehicles and control emissions more effectively.

Of course, individuals also have a responsibility.

However, I don't think it is realistic to simply tell people to stop using motorbikes when public transportation is not convenient enough.

The city needs to provide people with practical alternatives first.

Another issue is littering.

Unfortunately, I still see people throwing rubbish in public places or leaving rubbish on the street.

Sometimes, people may throw a plastic cup or a food container on the ground even when there is a rubbish bin nearby.

Personally, I find this quite frustrating because it is not really a difficult problem to understand.

People simply need to be more responsible.

However, I also think the city needs to improve rubbish collection and enforcement.

There should be more public bins in suitable locations, and people who deliberately litter should face reasonable penalties.

Education is also important, especially for children.

If people develop the habit of keeping public spaces clean from a young age, the situation may gradually improve.

The issue of sidewalks and streets being occupied illegally is another problem.

In many parts of Ho Chi Minh City, sidewalks are used for parking, street food stalls or small businesses.

I understand why people do this.

For some families, selling food or goods on the sidewalk may be an important source of income.

However, it creates serious problems for pedestrians.

Sometimes, there is almost no space left for people to walk, so they have to walk on the road.

This can be dangerous, especially in areas with heavy traffic.

I think the city needs to find a balance between keeping sidewalks clear and supporting people who depend on small businesses.

Simply removing street vendors without offering any alternatives may not solve the deeper problem.

Another issue that I think is particularly important is the condition of some canals and waterways.

Ho Chi Minh City has many canals and waterways, but some of them have become seriously polluted.

People sometimes throw rubbish into canals, and in some areas, the water can look and smell quite unpleasant.

I think this is a combination of poor waste management and a lack of public awareness.

The city needs to improve the wastewater system and strictly control illegal dumping.

At the same time, canals need to be cleaned and maintained regularly.

Personally, I think clean waterways could also make the city much more attractive.

They could become public spaces where people walk, exercise or simply relax.

Another thing I think the city needs to improve is the amount of green space.

Ho Chi Minh City is developing very quickly, and new buildings are constantly being constructed.

However, I think cities also need trees, parks and open spaces.

People need somewhere to relax and escape from traffic and concrete buildings.

Personally, I really appreciate parks and green spaces because I think they can have a positive effect on people's mental health.

In my opinion, city development should not simply mean building more apartments and shopping centres.

A good city should also be comfortable and pleasant to live in.

Overall, I think Ho Chi Minh City has made a lot of progress, but it is also facing several serious challenges.

The main problems, in my opinion, are traffic congestion, insufficient infrastructure, expensive housing, declining birth rates, air pollution, littering, sidewalk occupation and polluted canals.

I don't think any of these problems can be solved overnight.

The city needs better long-term planning, stronger public transportation, more affordable housing and stricter environmental management.

At the same time, ordinary citizens also need to take more responsibility for the city they live in.

Personally, I still love Ho Chi Minh City despite these problems.

I think it is a dynamic and exciting place with a lot of opportunities.

But because so many people choose to live here, the city needs to develop in a more organised and sustainable way.

For me, the goal should not simply be to make Ho Chi Minh City bigger.

It should be to make it a better and more comfortable place for ordinary people to live in.
