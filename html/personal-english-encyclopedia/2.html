A TYPICAL WEEKEND IN MY LIFE

When people ask me what I usually do at the weekend, I would say my weekends are generally quite relaxed.

I don't have a fixed schedule from morning to night, but I do have a few activities that I do quite regularly.

Since I work from Monday to Friday, the weekend is basically the time when I can slow down, spend time with my wife and do things that I actually enjoy.

Saturday is usually my more active day, mainly because I often play badminton in the morning.

I really enjoy playing badminton, and to be honest, it's probably one of my favourite ways to spend my free time.

I usually go to the badminton court in the morning because I think it's the best time to play.

The weather is generally more comfortable, and I feel much more energetic after a good night's sleep.

I normally play with friends, and we usually play doubles rather than singles.

What I like about badminton is that it's both physically demanding and quite social.

You have to move around constantly, react quickly and pay attention to your partner and your opponents.

Even though it looks like a fairly simple sport, it can actually be extremely tiring if you play several games in a row.

Sometimes, we say that we're just going to play for an hour or two, but we end up staying much longer than planned.

We might start with a few warm-up games, and then gradually become more competitive.

Personally, I enjoy the competitive side of badminton quite a lot.

I don't necessarily need to win every single game, but I definitely enjoy trying to improve and beat my friends.

I think another reason I like badminton is that it gives me a complete break from work.

When I'm on the court, I can't really think about programming, deadlines or problems at work.

I have to focus on the shuttlecock and react to what's happening in front of me.

So, even though I feel physically tired afterwards, I usually feel mentally refreshed.

After playing badminton, I normally go home, take a shower and have something to eat.

Sometimes I feel so hungry after playing that I can eat much more than I normally do.

I think that's one of the best things about exercising, although it can also be a little dangerous if you're trying to control your diet.

Once I'm back home, I often spend some time taking care of my aquarium.

I have a fairly large planted aquarium on my balcony, so maintaining it is actually one of my weekend hobbies.

I usually check the water, look at the plants and make sure everything is working properly.

Depending on the condition of the aquarium, I may also do a water change or clean some parts of the equipment.

It can be quite time-consuming, but I genuinely enjoy it.

I think taking care of an aquarium is a very relaxing hobby.

You have to be patient and pay attention to small details.

When I sit there and look at the fish swimming around and the plants growing, I feel much calmer.

It's completely different from spending time in front of a computer.

I also take care of the plants on my balcony.

I have several pots there, so I usually check whether they need watering or whether any leaves need to be removed.

Since Ho Chi Minh City is quite hot, I have to pay attention to the weather and make sure I don't overwater them.

To be honest, I'm not an expert gardener at all.

I've actually had a few plants die because I didn't take care of them properly.

But I still enjoy growing plants.

There's something quite satisfying about seeing a plant gradually grow after you've taken care of it for a long time.

In the afternoon, I often have some time to relax at home.

Sometimes I sit at my PC and play games or simply browse the internet.

I also spend quite a lot of time on my phone, just scrolling through social media or watching short videos.

I know spending too much time on my phone isn't necessarily the most productive thing to do, but I think everyone needs some completely lazy time at the weekend.

During the working week, I spend a lot of time concentrating on my job, so sometimes I just want to turn off my brain and do absolutely nothing.

Of course, I don't spend the entire weekend at home.

My wife and I often go out together.

One of our most common destinations is AEON Mall Bình Tân.

We might go there to do some shopping, have a meal or simply walk around.

Sometimes we also go to GO An Lạc because it's convenient for buying groceries.

We don't always have a specific shopping list.

Quite often, we just go there to buy a few things and then end up buying much more than we originally planned.

Eating out is also a pretty regular part of our weekend.

My wife and I don't always go to expensive restaurants.

Sometimes we simply eat at a local, affordable restaurant.

Other times, we might go to a nicer restaurant or even have a buffet.

I think the type of food we choose depends on our mood and our budget.

If we are tired, we usually choose something convenient and familiar.

But if we want to treat ourselves, we might go somewhere a little more special.

I also occasionally meet my friends for coffee or bubble tea.

We may sit in a café for a couple of hours, talk about work and life, and just catch up with each other.

I think this is quite important because when everyone gets busy with work and personal responsibilities, it can be surprisingly difficult to find time to meet friends.

Another thing I enjoy doing is simply walking around the apartment complex.

Since Vision has quite a lot of open space, I can take a walk around the park or the central area in the evening.

I sometimes go with my wife, but I also enjoy walking alone.

It's a very simple activity, but I think it's a good way to clear my head.

There are usually families, children and other residents outside, so the atmosphere is quite lively.

At the same time, it's still much more peaceful than the busy streets of Ho Chi Minh City.

On Sunday, my routine is usually a little more relaxed.

I tend to wake up later and take my time in the morning.

I might have breakfast, check my aquarium and plants, and then decide what I want to do for the rest of the day.

Sometimes my wife and I go to a city park for a walk.

I like going to parks because they give me a chance to get away from buildings and traffic for a while.

We may simply walk around, sit on a bench or buy something to drink.

And every now and then, we go back to my hometown for the weekend.

We don't do this every week, of course, because travelling takes time and requires a bit of planning.

But whenever we go, I really enjoy the change of atmosphere.

Life in Ho Chi Minh City is busy and quite noisy, while my hometown is much quieter and more relaxed.

So going back there feels like a short break from city life.

I can spend time with my family, eat home-cooked food and simply enjoy a slower pace of life.

Overall, I would say my weekends are a mixture of exercise, hobbies, time at home and spending time with my wife and friends.

I don't usually have a very exciting or luxurious weekend, but I actually like it that way.

After working five days a week, I don't necessarily need to travel somewhere expensive or do something extraordinary.

Sometimes, playing badminton in the morning, taking care of my aquarium, eating out with my wife or simply relaxing at my PC is more than enough.

So, if I had to describe my typical weekend in one sentence, I would say it's a balance between staying active and doing absolutely nothing.

And honestly, I think that's exactly what I need after a busy working week in Ho Chi Minh City.

ĐÀ LẠT TRIP

One of the trips I really enjoyed taking with my wife was a trip to Da Lat.

It wasn't an extremely long or luxurious holiday, but I think it was a really memorable experience because it was one of those trips where we could genuinely slow down and enjoy spending time together.

The trip actually started quite a while before we left, when my wife and I began discussing where we should go.

Since we both work from Monday to Friday, we didn't want to choose somewhere that was too far away or required too much complicated planning.

At first, we considered several different destinations, but eventually we settled on Da Lat.

I think the main reason was the weather.

Ho Chi Minh City is hot almost all year round, so the idea of going somewhere with a much cooler climate was very attractive to us.

We also thought Da Lat would be a good destination for a couple.

There are plenty of cafés, restaurants and tourist attractions, and we didn't necessarily have to plan every minute of the trip.

We could simply explore the city at a relatively relaxed pace.

Once we had decided on Da Lat, the next thing we had to do was find somewhere to stay.

We spent quite a bit of time looking at different hotels and homestays online.

We checked the photos, the location, the reviews and, of course, the price.

Personally, I prefer choosing accommodation that is reasonably comfortable and conveniently located.

I don't necessarily need a five-star hotel, but I don't want to stay somewhere too far from the main attractions either.

My wife, on the other hand, usually pays more attention to the appearance of the place.

She likes accommodation that looks nice and has a pleasant atmosphere.

After comparing several options, we eventually chose a place that seemed comfortable and was in a convenient location.

We also made sure that it had good reviews because I think online reviews can be quite useful when you're choosing accommodation in a city you've never visited before.

After booking the accommodation, we bought our bus tickets.

We decided to travel by coach rather than fly because it was more convenient and, in our opinion, more suitable for a relatively short trip.

The preparation before the trip was actually quite interesting.

My wife and I had to decide what clothes to bring because Da Lat is much cooler than Ho Chi Minh City.

I had to pack some long-sleeved shirts and a jacket, which I don't normally need in my daily life.

We also prepared some basic personal items, chargers and other things we might need during the trip.

To be honest, I tend to pack quite practically.

I don't like bringing too many things because I find carrying a huge suitcase inconvenient.

My wife is slightly different.

She usually spends more time checking whether we've packed everything we need.

Sometimes, she reminds me about things that I completely forgot to bring.

So, I would say preparing for a trip is a small team effort between the two of us.

On the day we left, we travelled to the bus station and waited for our coach.

I was actually quite excited because I hadn't been to Da Lat for a while.

Once we got on the bus, the journey began.

The bus ride was fairly long, but I didn't find it too boring.

I spent some time looking out of the window, listening to podcasts and using my phone.

My wife also slept for part of the journey.

As we gradually moved away from Ho Chi Minh City, the scenery started to change.

The roads became less crowded, and the landscape became more rural.

I think this is one of the nice things about travelling by coach.

You can actually see the scenery changing along the way instead of simply arriving at your destination by plane.

When we finally arrived in Da Lat, the first thing I noticed was the weather.

It was noticeably cooler and more comfortable than Ho Chi Minh City.

Honestly, just stepping outside and feeling the cool air already made me feel like I was on holiday.

After checking into our accommodation and taking a short break, we started exploring the city.

We didn't want to spend the whole first day just resting, so we went out and walked around some of the more popular areas.

One of the things I really enjoyed about Da Lat was simply exploring the city by motorbike.

The roads can be a little winding and hilly, but the scenery is very different from what I'm used to in Ho Chi Minh City.

There are pine trees, hills and a lot of green space.

In Ho Chi Minh City, I constantly see buildings, traffic and busy roads.

In Da Lat, the atmosphere feels much more relaxed and natural.

Of course, we visited Xuan Huong Lake.

I think it's one of the places that almost every tourist visits in Da Lat.

We walked around the lake, took some photos and simply enjoyed the cool weather.

We also visited the Da Lat Night Market.

It was quite crowded, but I actually enjoyed the atmosphere.

There were a lot of food stalls and small shops selling clothes, souvenirs and local products.

I think the night market is one of the best places to experience the more lively side of Da Lat.

During the day, the city can feel quite peaceful, but at night, the market becomes much more energetic.

We also visited some cafés during the trip.

I think café culture is a very important part of the Da Lat tourism experience.

There are so many cafés with different designs and views.

Some cafés are built on hills, so you can look out over the city.

Others are surrounded by trees and have a very peaceful atmosphere.

My wife particularly enjoyed visiting cafés because she likes taking photos and experiencing places with a nice atmosphere.

Personally, I also enjoyed it, although I probably don't need to visit five different cafés in one trip.

For me, one good cup of coffee and a comfortable seat are usually enough.

Besides cafés, we visited some tourist attractions and scenic areas around the city.

We went to places with beautiful views and spent time taking photos.

We also visited some flower gardens and areas with a lot of pine trees.

One thing I found interesting was that many tourist attractions in Da Lat are quite photo-friendly.

You can often find people taking pictures everywhere.

To be honest, my wife took many more photos than I did, but I was happy to help her take pictures.

We also tried some local food during the trip.

One of the dishes I really enjoyed was Da Lat-style grilled rice paper, which is often described as Vietnamese pizza.

It's quite simple, but it's really tasty, especially when you eat it hot.

We also had hot soy milk at night.

I think it tasted especially good because of the cold weather.

In Ho Chi Minh City, I don't usually think about drinking something hot at night, but in Da Lat, it felt completely natural.

Of course, we also tried other local dishes and snacks.

We ate grilled food, hotpot and different street foods.

Personally, I think hotpot is particularly suitable for Da Lat because the weather is cool.

Sitting in a warm restaurant and having a hot meal is a very enjoyable experience.

One of the most interesting things about the trip was simply the change of pace.

In Ho Chi Minh City, I usually have to think about work, traffic and my daily schedule.

In Da Lat, I didn't feel the same pressure.

My wife and I could wake up, have breakfast, decide where to go and simply enjoy the day.

We didn't have to rush from one place to another.

Even when we were just walking around or sitting in a café, I felt that we were actually relaxing.

However, I don't think Da Lat is perfect as a tourist destination.

One problem is the traffic, especially during weekends and public holidays.

The city is relatively small, so when there are too many tourists, the roads can become extremely crowded.

Parking can also be a problem in popular areas.

Sometimes, there are so many motorbikes and cars that simply getting to a tourist attraction can be quite stressful.

I also think some tourist attractions could be better managed and maintained.

In certain places, there can be too much litter or too many businesses trying to attract tourists.

In my opinion, Da Lat needs to find a better balance between tourism development and protecting its natural atmosphere.

Another issue is that the city can become very crowded during peak seasons.

I understand that tourism is extremely important for the local economy, but I think the city should focus more on sustainable tourism in the long term.

After spending a few days in Da Lat, it was finally time to go back to Ho Chi Minh City.

Preparing to leave was actually a little sad.

We had to pack our clothes, check the room carefully and make sure we hadn't forgotten anything.

My wife also bought a few souvenirs and local products, so we had slightly more luggage than when we arrived.

I think that's quite typical.

You go on holiday with one suitcase and somehow come back with several extra bags.

Before getting on the bus, we had one last meal and bought some snacks for the journey.

Then we went to the bus station and started our journey back to the city.

The trip home felt quite different from the trip there.

I was more tired, so I spent a lot of time resting and looking out of the window.

As we got closer to Ho Chi Minh City, the traffic gradually became heavier and the weather became hotter.

I remember thinking that I was already missing Da Lat a little bit.

At the same time, I was also looking forward to getting home and sleeping in my own bed.

Overall, I really enjoyed that trip to Da Lat with my wife.

What I liked most was not necessarily one particular tourist attraction.

I think it was the whole experience, from choosing the destination and booking the accommodation to travelling by bus, exploring the city, trying local food and simply spending relaxed time together.

For me, Da Lat is a great destination for people who want to take a short break from the busy lifestyle of a big city.

It has some problems, especially traffic and overcrowding during peak periods, but I still think it is one of the most enjoyable places to visit in Vietnam.

So, if I had the chance, I would definitely go back to Da Lat again.

Next time, I might try to stay for a little longer and explore some less touristy places instead of just visiting the most popular attractions.

MY WORKDAY

I work as a web developer for a Japanese company in Vietnam, and I would say a typical working day for me is a mixture of coding, analysing requirements, communicating with other people and dealing with unexpected problems.

I usually work from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m., from Monday to Friday.

My job is mainly related to web development.

I work on both the frontend and the backend, although I would say I'm more specialised in frontend development.

I work for an outsourcing company, so the projects I work on are usually for clients or teams in Japan.

This means that apart from technical skills, communication and understanding requirements are also quite important.

My working day usually starts when I arrive at the office in the morning.

I normally check my computer, open the tools I use for work and look at the tasks assigned to me.

Usually, I don't simply start coding immediately.

The first thing I need to do is understand the task properly.

I read the requirements carefully and try to figure out exactly what the client or the team wants.

Sometimes, a task looks very simple at first, but after I analyse it, I realise that there are actually several different parts involved.

For example, it may require changes to the user interface, some backend logic and database-related work.

So, I usually spend some time thinking about the task before touching the code.

I try to understand the existing system and check how the current feature works.

If I don't fully understand something, I may ask a colleague or discuss it with the team.

Another important part of my work is estimating tasks.

We often need to estimate how much time or effort a task might require.

Personally, I think estimating is actually quite difficult because software development is not always predictable.

Sometimes, you think a task will take two or three hours, but then you discover an unexpected problem in the existing code.

Suddenly, the task becomes much more complicated.

So, when I estimate a task, I try to consider not only the coding itself but also the time needed for analysis, testing and fixing possible problems.

I don't think a good estimate means simply guessing a number.

You need to understand the task first.

Once I have a clear understanding of the requirements, I start working on the task.

As I mentioned, I mainly work on the frontend, so I spend a lot of time building or modifying user interfaces.

For example, I may work with React or Next.js and deal with things like components, forms, data fetching and user interactions.

I also need to think about how the interface should behave in different situations.

However, I also work on the backend from time to time.

I may need to create or modify APIs, handle data or investigate problems between the frontend and the backend.

Actually, one thing I like about working on both sides is that I can understand the whole flow of a feature better.

I don't just think about what the user sees on the screen.

I can also understand what happens behind the scenes.

Of course, a large part of my working day is also spent debugging.

I think debugging is probably one of the most unavoidable parts of being a developer.

Sometimes, I get a bug report and I have to investigate why something isn't working correctly.

I may check the browser, look at the network requests, read the logs or trace the code step by step.

The frustrating thing is that the actual fix can sometimes take only ten minutes, but finding the cause of the problem may take several hours.

There are also times when I feel completely stuck.

I look at the code and think, "Why is this happening?" I try one solution, then another one, but nothing seems to work.

When that happens, I usually take a short break or discuss the problem with a colleague.

Sometimes, simply explaining the problem to another person helps me understand it more clearly.

I also use AI quite a lot in my daily work.

I think AI has become a very useful assistant for developers.

I may use it to explain unfamiliar code, suggest possible solutions or help me think of different approaches to a problem.

However, I don't simply copy and paste everything AI gives me.

I still need to understand the code and check whether the solution is actually suitable for the project.

Personally, I think the ability to work with AI is becoming an important skill for developers.

It's not really about letting AI do everything for you.

It's more about using AI to work more efficiently.

Apart from coding, I also have to attend meetings from time to time.

We may have meetings to discuss requirements, project progress or technical problems.

Sometimes, I think meetings are useful because they help everyone understand the same goal.

But if there are too many meetings, they can also interrupt my concentration.

Personally, I prefer having a few focused meetings rather than constantly being interrupted during the day.

When I'm deeply focused on coding, it can take quite a while to get back into the same mental state after a meeting.

Another part of my job is supporting my colleagues.

For example, if someone has a problem with a feature or doesn't understand part of the system, I may help them investigate it.

Sometimes, my colleagues also help me.

I think this kind of cooperation is extremely important in software development.

Even if you're a good developer, you can't realistically know everything.

There are always new technologies, unfamiliar codebases and problems that you've never seen before.

So, I think being able to ask for help and support other people is a very important part of working in a team.

At some point during the morning, I usually have a cup of coffee.

Our company has coffee available, so I can simply make a cup at the office.

To be honest, coffee is not absolutely essential for me, but I do enjoy drinking it while working.

Sometimes, taking a few minutes to make coffee is actually a nice mental break.

Around lunchtime, I take a break and have lunch.

I usually try not to think too much about work during lunch because I think it's important to give my brain a proper break.

Sometimes, I chat with my colleagues about everyday things.

We may talk about food, traffic or something interesting that happened recently.

I think these casual conversations are actually quite important.

If people only talk about tasks and deadlines, the working environment can become quite stressful.

After lunch, I normally return to my tasks.

Depending on the day, I may continue coding or work on testing and fixing bugs.

Testing is another important part of development.

After implementing a feature, I need to check whether it works as expected.

I also need to think about different situations that might cause problems.

For example, I may test what happens if a user enters invalid information or if the server returns an error.

Sometimes, developers only think about the ideal situation, but real users don't always behave in the way we expect.

Once I feel that a task is ready, I may deploy it to a development or staging server.

I don't have permission to deploy directly to production, so production deployment is handled by people with the appropriate access.

Before deploying, I usually make sure the changes are ready and check whether there are any obvious problems.

After deployment, I may test the feature again in the new environment.

Sometimes, a task works perfectly on my local computer but behaves differently on the development or staging server.

This can be quite frustrating because then I have to investigate the differences between the environments.

Near the end of the day, I usually check the status of my tasks and think about what I need to do next.

If I haven't finished a task, I try to make sure I understand the current situation clearly so I can continue more efficiently the next day.

I don't always finish everything I planned to do in one day.

In software development, unexpected problems can easily change your schedule.

To be honest, the most mentally tiring part of my job is probably having to concentrate for a long time.

Coding requires a lot of mental energy, especially when you're trying to understand a complicated problem.

There are days when I feel very productive and solve several problems quite quickly.

But there are also days when I spend hours dealing with one difficult issue.

I think that's one of the things people outside the software industry may not fully understand.

They may think that developers simply sit in front of a computer and type code all day.

In reality, a lot of our time is spent thinking, analysing and trying to understand problems.

Overall, I would say my typical working day is quite busy but also interesting.

I spend a lot of time coding, but my job is not only about writing code.

I also need to analyse requirements, estimate tasks, communicate with colleagues, attend meetings, test features and sometimes deal with unexpected problems.

I think that's what makes web development interesting to me.

Every day is not exactly the same.

Even if I work on similar types of tasks, the problems are often different.

So, if I had to describe my working day in one sentence, I would say it's basically a combination of coding, problem-solving and constantly trying to figure out why something isn't working the way it should.

MY JOB'S HARDEST PART

When people ask me about the most difficult part of my job as a web developer, I honestly don't think I can give just one simple answer.

There are several different challenges, and they can become especially difficult when they happen at the same time.

However, if I had to choose the biggest challenge, I would probably say it's dealing with unexpected problems, especially when I'm working under time pressure.

The first thing that comes to mind is debugging.

I think debugging is probably one of the most frustrating parts of being a developer.

When I start a task, I usually have a general idea of what I need to do.

I expect to spend a certain amount of time analysing the requirements and writing code.

But sometimes, I suddenly discover a bug that I didn't expect at all.

The frustrating thing is that the actual solution may be very simple, but finding the cause of the problem can take hours.

For example, I may notice that a feature is not working correctly.

At first, I check the frontend code.

Then I check the API.

After that, I look at the network requests and the logs.

Sometimes, everything seems to be working normally, but the problem is still there.

At that point, I have to investigate the problem step by step.

I try to reproduce the bug and understand exactly when it happens.

Sometimes, I change one small thing and suddenly the problem disappears.

But then I have to ask myself why it worked in the first place.

I don't want to simply fix a bug by accident.

I need to understand the root cause.

I think that's the biggest mental challenge of debugging.

You have to keep asking yourself, "What exactly is happening here?" and "What am I missing?" Another difficult part of my job is working with legacy code.

In simple terms, legacy code is old code that has been in a system for a long time.

The problem is that the person who originally wrote the code may no longer be working on the project.

Sometimes, there is very little documentation, so you have to understand the system by reading the code itself.

Personally, I find this quite challenging because the code may not follow the style or structure I would normally use.

There may be unusual workarounds or complicated logic that seems strange at first.

But I can't simply rewrite everything from scratch.

The system is already being used, and changing one part may affect several other parts.

So, I have to be extremely careful.

I usually spend quite a lot of time trying to understand the existing code before making any changes.

Sometimes, I think working with legacy code is a little like exploring an old house.

You open one door and discover another room that you didn't know existed.

Then you realise that the room is connected to three other parts of the house.

And honestly, some parts of a codebase can feel exactly like that.

Unclear requirements are another major challenge.

Sometimes, a task is described in only a few sentences, but the actual development work is much more complicated.

For example, a requirement may say that a certain feature should be added or that the user experience should be improved.

But as a developer, I need to know exactly how the feature is supposed to behave.

What happens if the user enters invalid information? What happens if the API fails? What should happen on a mobile device? These are the kinds of questions that may not be clearly explained in the original requirement.

When the requirements are unclear, I usually try to analyse the task and identify the parts that need clarification.

Then I may discuss them with my colleagues or ask for more information.

I think this is extremely important because starting to code too early can create bigger problems later.

You may spend several hours implementing something, only to discover that you misunderstood the requirement.

And honestly, that can be quite frustrating.

Deadlines can also make everything more difficult.

Normally, I prefer working in a calm and organised way.

I like to understand the task properly, plan my work and then start coding.

However, when there is a tight deadline, I have to work much faster.

The problem is that software development is not always predictable.

A task may look simple, but then an unexpected bug appears.

If the deadline doesn't change, I have to find a way to deal with the problem within a limited amount of time.

Personally, I think the most stressful situation is when several difficult things happen at the same time.

For example, the requirements are not completely clear, the existing code is complicated and the deadline is approaching.

In that situation, I have to prioritise very carefully.

I try to identify what is absolutely necessary and what can possibly be improved later.

I think one important lesson I've learned is that a perfect solution is not always realistic.

Sometimes, you need to find a practical solution that solves the immediate problem while keeping the code maintainable.

Another thing that has changed my work quite a lot is AI.

I use AI as a kind of assistant in my daily development work.

For example, I may ask AI to explain unfamiliar code, suggest possible causes of a bug or give me different approaches to solving a problem.

I think AI can save a lot of time, especially during the initial investigation stage.

Sometimes, I have a problem but I don't even know where to start.

AI can help me organise my thoughts and suggest some possible directions.

However, I don't think AI completely removes the difficult parts of software development.

The biggest problem is that AI can sometimes give you a solution that looks reasonable but is actually wrong or unsuitable for your project.

So, I still have to understand the code and check the solution carefully.

I can't simply copy and paste whatever AI suggests.

Personally, I think using AI effectively is also a skill.

You need to ask good questions, provide enough context and understand the answer you receive.

In some ways, AI has changed the type of problems I deal with.

In the past, I might spend a long time searching for information online.

Now, I can get a quick explanation from AI.

But I still need to make the final decision as a developer.

I also think communication can be surprisingly difficult.

As a developer, I sometimes understand a technical problem very clearly in my own head, but explaining it to another person is a different challenge.

I need to explain the problem in a way that other people can understand, especially if they are not directly involved in the code.

If I explain things badly, people may misunderstand the situation, and that can create even more problems.

So, I think communication is an important skill that developers sometimes underestimate.

Overall, I would say the most difficult part of my job is not simply writing code.

In fact, writing the code is often the easier part once I understand the problem clearly.

The real challenge is understanding complicated systems, dealing with unclear information, finding the root cause of unexpected problems and making good decisions under time pressure.

There are definitely days when I feel mentally exhausted.

Sometimes, I spend most of the day trying to solve one problem and still don't feel completely satisfied with the result.

However, I also think that's what makes the job interesting.

When I finally understand a difficult bug or solve a problem that has been bothering me for hours, I feel a real sense of satisfaction.

So, if I had to summarise the most difficult part of my job, I would say it's the constant uncertainty.

You never completely know what problem is going to appear next.

But at the same time, I think that's exactly what keeps web development challenging and interesting for me.

WHY I CHOSE TO BECOME DEVELOPER

When people ask me why I chose to become a web developer, I honestly have to say that it wasn't the result of one single decision or one specific moment in my life.

It was more like a gradual process.

I became interested in computers when I was quite young, and over time, that interest eventually turned into a career.

When I was a child, I spent quite a lot of time around computers.

Back then, I often went to Internet cafés, which were extremely popular in Vietnam.

I played games, browsed the internet and generally enjoyed using computers.

Of course, at that time, I had absolutely no idea that I would eventually become a developer.

I just thought computers were interesting.

I was curious about how they worked and I enjoyed spending time with them.

I think another reason I became interested in the IT industry was the way people talked about it.

When I was younger, I often heard people say that IT was a promising career and that developers could earn a relatively high salary.

To be honest, money was definitely one of the factors that influenced my thinking.

I don't think there's anything wrong with admitting that.

When you're choosing a career, salary and financial stability are obviously important.

At that time, I thought becoming a developer might be a good way to build a stable career.

However, I don't think money alone would have been enough to keep me in this industry for so many years.

At some point, I started learning more seriously about computers and programming.

I began to realise that programming was not simply about using a computer.

It was about creating something and solving problems.

I found that quite interesting.

For example, when you write some code and it doesn't work, you have to figure out why.

You have to look at the problem, think logically and try different solutions.

Sometimes, it can be extremely frustrating.

But when you finally understand the problem and fix it, you get a very satisfying feeling.

I think that feeling was one of the things that gradually made me more interested in programming.

I eventually decided to focus on web development.

One reason was that I found it relatively easy to see the result of my work.

When you build a website or a web application, you can actually see the interface in front of you.

You can click buttons, fill in forms and interact with the product.

Personally, I found that quite motivating.

I liked the idea of creating something that people could actually use.

I started learning different technologies and gradually became more familiar with frontend development.

I learned about HTML, CSS and JavaScript, and later moved on to frameworks and more advanced technologies.

Over time, I also started learning about backend development.

Although I still consider myself more of a frontend developer, I now work with both the frontend and the backend.

I think understanding both sides has been quite useful for me.

When I work on a feature, I can think about the whole process rather than only focusing on what the user sees on the screen.

My career path hasn't always been completely smooth.

Like many developers, I had to spend a lot of time learning things by myself.

The IT industry changes very quickly, so what you learn today may not be enough a few years later.

New frameworks, libraries and tools are constantly being introduced.

When I first started learning web development, I had to search for information online, read documentation and watch tutorials.

Sometimes, I spent a lot of time trying to solve a very simple problem.

I think that was quite difficult at the beginning because there were so many things to learn.

However, one thing I gradually realised is that developers don't need to know everything.

What matters is being able to learn and solve problems.

This is actually one of the things I like about the job.

I don't have to memorise every single piece of information.

If I encounter something unfamiliar, I can investigate it and learn what I need.

At the moment, I work as a web developer for an outsourcing company in Vietnam that works with Japanese clients.

My work mainly involves frontend development, although I also deal with backend tasks from time to time.

I think the salary is obviously one of the positive aspects of the job.

Compared with many other careers, IT can offer relatively good earning potential, especially if you develop strong technical skills and gain experience.

But honestly, salary is no longer the only reason I stay in this career.

After working in the industry for quite a long time, I've realised that I also value the flexibility and the opportunities to keep learning.

The IT industry has changed dramatically since I first became interested in computers.

Technology is now involved in almost every part of our lives.

People use websites and applications for shopping, banking, entertainment and communication.

The demand for technology has grown significantly, and I think this has created a lot of opportunities for people working in IT.

At the same time, I think the industry has also become more competitive.

There are more developers now, and companies often expect people to have a wider range of skills.

A developer may need to understand not only programming but also databases, cloud services, security and system architecture.

And of course, artificial intelligence has changed the industry quite a lot recently.

Personally, I use AI as a kind of assistant in my daily work.

It can help me understand unfamiliar code, suggest possible solutions and speed up certain tasks.

Some people worry that AI will completely replace developers.

Personally, I don't think it's quite that simple.

I think AI may replace some repetitive tasks, but developers still need to understand problems, make decisions and check whether a solution is actually suitable.

In fact, I think the role of a developer may gradually change.

Instead of simply writing code line by line, developers may need to focus more on understanding the overall problem and using technology effectively.

So, looking back, I would say I chose web development for a combination of reasons.

I was interested in computers from a young age, I was influenced by the idea that IT was a well-paid career and I gradually discovered that I actually enjoyed problem-solving.

I also like the fact that the industry is constantly changing.

It can be tiring because I always have something new to learn, but I think it also prevents the job from becoming completely boring.

If I had to be completely honest, I probably didn't have a very clear career plan when I was younger.

I didn't grow up dreaming of becoming a web developer.

I simply liked computers, I saw IT as a promising career and I gradually found my way into the industry.

And now, after working as a developer for several years, I think it was actually a pretty good choice for me.

I still have difficult days, and I sometimes get frustrated with debugging or complicated projects.

But overall, I think web development suits the way I think and the kind of work I enjoy doing.
