MY APARTMENT

Overall, I'm really happy with it, and I would say it's a pretty comfortable place to live.

The remaining six buildings are still only at the foundation stage, so I think it'll be quite a while before the entire project is finished.
Once the whole project is completed, there will be ten buildings altogether.
I live on the ninth floor of Building A, which is one of the 25-storey buildings.

After living there for about a year, I've gradually become quite attached to the place.

I think that's a really good facility, especially considering how hot the weather can be in Ho Chi Minh City.

Thankfully, most of them have been cleared away, so getting around is much easier now.
I think these changes have made the neighbourhood feel more organised and more comfortable than it was when I first moved in.
Another thing I appreciate is the security.
There are quite a lot of security guards around the complex, and they seem to do their job very well.
They're usually present at the entrances and in different areas of the complex, so I generally feel quite safe living there.
For me, that's especially important because I often come home after work in the evening.
Of course, living there isn't perfect, and there are a couple of practical issues that residents often talk about.

The complex only has one basement parking area.

Last time I heard, each list had more than a hundred people waiting for a parking slot.
So, parking is definitely one of the major weaknesses of the apartment complex.
There are also a few minor problems with insects.
Because the complex has a lot of open space and greenery, there are quite a lot of mosquitoes around the park in the late afternoon.
On rainy days, rove beetles sometimes fly into the apartment through the balcony or windows.
So I have to be a little careful and make sure they don't get inside.
But honestly, these are relatively minor issues compared with the advantages.
Overall, I really enjoy living in Vision.
I like the spacious layout, the facilities and the convenient location.
More importantly, I feel that the area is gradually developing, and the quality of life is improving over time.
So, if I had to describe my home in one sentence, I would say it's not a perfect place, but it's a comfortable and convenient place where I can genuinely see myself living for quite a long time.

MY CITY

Well, I come from Ho Chi Minh City, which is the largest and probably the most dynamic city in Vietnam.
I think it's quite difficult to describe the city in just a few words because it has so many different sides to it.
On the one hand, it's modern, energetic and full of opportunities.
But on the other hand, it can also be extremely crowded, noisy and stressful, especially during rush hour.
As far as I know, Ho Chi Minh City has a population of around ten million people, or even more if we count people who come here from other provinces to study and work.
That's one of the things that makes the city feel so lively.
Wherever you go, there are always people around you.
The city is also quite large in terms of area, and it's made up of many different districts, each with its own atmosphere.
For example, District 1 is usually considered the city centre and is quite modern and tourist-friendly, while areas like Bình Tân, where I live, are more residential and practical.
Personally, I live in Bình Tân District, in an apartment.
What I like about my neighbourhood is that it is quite convenient for daily life.
There are supermarkets, convenience stores, cafés and restaurants within a relatively short distance.
My apartment complex itself has some useful facilities, such as a swimming pool, a small park and sports areas.
So, although I don't live right in the city centre, I still have almost everything I need for my everyday life.
There is also a large shopping centre not too far away, and I can easily get to supermarkets like WinMart or Co.op Food.
In terms of healthcare, there are hospitals and medical facilities in the surrounding area, so I don't really have to travel across the whole city whenever I need basic medical services.
To be honest, that's one of the reasons I prefer living in a residential area rather than right in the centre.
It's a little less exciting, but it's much more practical.
One of the most obvious characteristics of Ho Chi Minh City is the traffic.
I think traffic is probably the first thing many people notice when they visit the city.
There are millions of motorbikes on the roads, and during peak hours, the streets can become incredibly crowded.
In the morning, people are rushing to work and school, and in the late afternoon, everyone seems to be heading home at the same time.
Traffic jams are a pretty common problem.
Sometimes, a journey that normally takes about thirty minutes can take more than an hour.
Personally, I experience this quite regularly on my way home from work.
The traffic is especially frustrating when it rains because the roads become even more congested.
In some areas, flooding can also make the situation worse.
Of course, there are some public facilities and major buildings that make Ho Chi Minh City quite attractive.
For example, we have large shopping malls, modern office buildings and many public parks.
In the city centre, there are also some famous historical and cultural landmarks, such as the Independence Palace, the Saigon Central Post Office and Notre-Dame Cathedral Basilica of Saigon.
These places are quite popular with tourists because they reflect different periods of the city's history.
If someone asked me to recommend tourist attractions, I would probably mention Bui Vien Street and Nguyen Hue Walking Street.
They have completely different atmospheres.
Bui Vien is famous for its nightlife and is usually very lively, especially in the evening.
Nguyen Hue Walking Street, on the other hand, is a much more open public space where people can walk around, take photos and enjoy events.
I also think the city has a lot of interesting cafés, and café culture is actually a big part of daily life in Ho Chi Minh City.
However, I don't want to give the impression that the city is perfect.
One major problem is pollution.
Air pollution can be quite noticeable, especially on busy roads with heavy traffic.
Noise pollution is another issue.
Sometimes, the city is simply too noisy, with motorbike engines, car horns and construction work happening almost everywhere.
Another problem is that Ho Chi Minh City is becoming increasingly crowded.
As more and more people move here for work and education, the pressure on infrastructure is also increasing.
Housing prices are quite high, traffic is getting worse and some public services are under pressure.
I think the city has developed very quickly, but in some ways, its infrastructure hasn't always been able to keep up with that growth.
Despite all these problems, I still really like Ho Chi Minh City.
I think the biggest strength of the city is its energy.
People here tend to be quite hardworking and ambitious, and there are always new businesses, restaurants or projects appearing.
The city never really feels completely quiet.
Even late at night, you can still find people eating, working or simply hanging out with friends.
So, to sum up, Ho Chi Minh City is a crowded, busy and sometimes chaotic place, but it's also dynamic and full of opportunities.
For me, it's not just a place where I live.
It's the city where I work, spend most of my time and build my daily life.
And although I sometimes complain about the traffic and pollution, I think I would still miss the city quite a lot if I had to live somewhere else.

TRANSPORTATION

When it comes to transportation, I think Ho Chi Minh City is a very interesting city to talk about because the transportation system has changed quite a lot during the time I've been living here.
I've lived in the city for many years, and from my personal experience, transportation is both one of the most essential parts of daily life and one of the biggest challenges for people living here.
The first thing that probably comes to mind when people talk about transportation in Ho Chi Minh City is motorbikes.
Motorbikes are by far the most common means of transport.
Almost every family owns at least one, and many people use them every single day to go to work, go shopping or simply get around the city.
Personally, I also use a motorbike quite regularly.
I think the main reason motorbikes are so popular is that they are relatively affordable and extremely flexible.
You can get through smaller streets and alleys, and you don't have to worry too much about finding a large parking space.
However, the huge number of motorbikes also creates a lot of problems.
The most obvious one is traffic congestion.
During rush hour, especially in the morning and late afternoon, many major roads become incredibly crowded.
Sometimes, you can see thousands of motorbikes moving slowly along the road, and the traffic seems to stretch as far as you can see.
I personally experience this quite often on my way home from work.
A journey that normally takes around thirty or forty minutes can sometimes take much longer during peak hours.
The situation becomes even worse when it rains.
People tend to drive more slowly, some roads become flooded, and traffic can come to a complete standstill in certain areas.
I think one of the reasons for this problem is that the city's infrastructure has not always been able to keep up with its rapid growth.
Ho Chi Minh City has grown incredibly quickly, and more and more people have moved here for work and education.
As a result, there are simply more vehicles on the road than the existing infrastructure can comfortably handle.
In terms of roads, I would say the city has a mixture of large main roads, smaller streets and narrow alleys.
Some major roads have been expanded or upgraded, but there are still plenty of bottlenecks.
In some areas, two relatively wide roads suddenly become much narrower, and this creates serious congestion.
Another issue is the condition of the roads.
I wouldn't say the roads are generally terrible, but there are definitely areas where the surface is uneven or where road construction has been going on for a long time.
Sometimes, you see several construction projects happening at the same time, and that can make travelling around the city quite frustrating.
One thing that I think deserves more attention is the use of pavements.
In theory, pavements are supposed to be for pedestrians.
But in reality, they are often used for parking motorbikes or for street businesses.
Some restaurants and shops put tables, chairs or other items on the pavement, while street vendors may also occupy part of the space.
This creates a real problem for pedestrians.
In some areas, people have no choice but to walk on the road because the pavement is completely blocked.
Personally, I think this is quite dangerous, especially when the roads are busy.
At the same time, I also understand why this problem is difficult to solve.
For many small businesses and street vendors, the pavement is closely connected to their livelihood.
Besides roads, Ho Chi Minh City also has a network of rivers, canals and smaller waterways.
The city has always been closely connected to water, and I think these waterways are a very important part of its geography.
However, for a long time, I don't think they were fully used as a transportation solution.
Some canals and rivers have been heavily polluted or surrounded by poorly planned areas.
In recent years, there has been more effort to improve the environment around some waterways, but I still think there is a lot of potential that hasn't been fully developed.
Personally, I think water transportation could become more useful in the future.
It probably cannot completely replace roads, but it could provide an alternative way to travel and reduce some pressure on the road system.
Of course, this would require a lot of investment and careful planning.
When it comes to rail transportation, I think the situation is quite interesting.
Vietnam has a railway system, and Ho Chi Minh City is connected to other parts of the country by train.
However, travelling by train is not usually the first choice for short-distance travel within the city.
For many years, people in Ho Chi Minh City have been waiting for a more developed urban rail system.
The metro is a major step in that direction.
I think the metro is particularly important because the city desperately needs more forms of public transportation that can carry a large number of people.
In my opinion, the metro could make a significant difference in the long term.
It has the potential to reduce the number of private vehicles on the roads and make commuting more predictable.
The problem is that building a large metro network takes a huge amount of time and money.
So I think the city needs to be patient, although people naturally want to see the system develop more quickly.
Public buses are another form of transportation in Ho Chi Minh City.
I have used buses before, and I think they have both advantages and disadvantages.
The biggest advantage is probably the price.
Bus fares are relatively affordable, so buses are a practical option for students and people who want to save money.
However, buses can be quite slow during rush hour because they are still affected by traffic congestion.
In addition, some people may find the bus routes less convenient compared with simply riding a motorbike.
For me, if I have somewhere specific to go and I am in a hurry, I would usually prefer using a motorbike.
I think this is one of the main reasons why public transportation is still not the first choice for many people.
It's not necessarily that people don't want to use public transport.
The problem is that private vehicles are often more convenient and flexible.
Another important part of transportation is air travel.
Ho Chi Minh City has a major international airport, and it plays a very important role in connecting the city with other parts of Vietnam and the world.
I think air travel has become much more common for Vietnamese people compared with the past.
However, the airport is also famous for being extremely busy.
During public holidays, the number of passengers can be overwhelming.
Travelling to the airport can also be stressful because the roads around it are often heavily congested.
So sometimes, the journey to the airport can be almost as stressful as the flight itself.
Overall, I think transportation in Ho Chi Minh City is a complicated issue.
The city has a huge number of motorbikes, increasing numbers of cars and a rapidly growing population.
At the same time, the infrastructure is still developing.
Personally, I don't think there is one single solution to the city's traffic problems.
The city needs better roads, a more extensive metro system and a more efficient public transportation network.
At the same time, I think there needs to be better management of pavements and more serious efforts to control illegal parking and the occupation of public spaces.
Despite all the problems, I have to admit that I've become quite used to the way transportation works here.
Sometimes, I complain about traffic jams, noise and crowded roads, but when I visit a quieter city, I actually find it strange that the streets are so empty.
So, if I had to sum up transportation in Ho Chi Minh City, I would say it's busy, chaotic and far from perfect, but it's also incredibly dynamic.
And as someone who has lived here for many years, I think transportation is probably one of the clearest reflections of the city's rapid growth and the challenges that come with it.

MY DAILY COMMUTE

My daily commute in Ho Chi Minh City is something I probably don't think about very much anymore because I've been doing it for years.
But if I actually stop and think about it, commuting takes up a surprisingly large part of my daily life.
I live in Vision Apartment in Binh Tan District, while my company is located on Nguyen Dinh Chieu Street in District 1.
So, every weekday, I have to travel from the western part of the city to the central area.
I usually go by motorbike, which is probably the most common way of getting around in Ho Chi Minh City.
On a normal working day, I leave home at around 7:30 in the morning.
My working hours start at 8, so I don't have a huge amount of time to spare.
Before leaving, I usually get dressed, have a quick breakfast and check the weather.
I think checking the weather is actually quite important in Ho Chi Minh City because the traffic situation can be completely different if it starts raining.
The first part of my journey is usually quite comfortable.
I leave my apartment complex and travel through the roads in Binh Tan.
Since I live in a residential area, the traffic is not always too bad at that time.
However, the situation gradually becomes more crowded as I get closer to the central districts.
One thing I really notice during my commute is how quickly the city becomes busy in the morning.
You can see thousands of people heading to work or school at the same time.
There are motorbikes everywhere, along with cars, buses and delivery vehicles.
Sometimes, it feels like the whole city is moving in the same direction.
Personally, I don't really enjoy driving in heavy traffic, but I've become quite used to it.
I think that's something that happens to most people who have lived in Ho Chi Minh City for a long time.
At first, traffic jams can feel extremely frustrating.
But after years of experiencing them, you gradually learn how to deal with the situation.
For me, the most difficult part is usually not the traffic itself, but the unpredictability.
On a normal day, I can get to work in around forty minutes.
But if there is an accident, heavy rain or an unusually serious traffic jam, the journey can take much longer.
So, even though my working hours start at eight, I try not to leave home too late.
If I leave just ten or fifteen minutes later than usual, I can sometimes feel the difference quite clearly.
The roads become much more crowded, and I have to spend more time waiting at traffic lights or moving slowly through congested areas.
Another thing that makes commuting difficult is the weather.
Ho Chi Minh City is quite hot, and riding a motorbike for a long time under the sun can be exhausting.
By the time I arrive at work, I sometimes feel a little sweaty and tired, especially during the hottest months.
Rain is even worse.
If it starts raining heavily, I usually have to find somewhere to stop and put on a raincoat.
And if the rain continues for a long time, the traffic can become absolutely chaotic.
Some roads get flooded, people drive more slowly and traffic jams become much more serious.
To be honest, rainy days are probably my least favourite days to commute.
You have to pay more attention to the road, visibility is worse and there are usually a lot more vehicles around you.
Sometimes, I arrive at work feeling as if I've already had a very tiring day, even though it's only eight in the morning.
My journey home is usually even more difficult than my morning commute.
I normally leave work at around five in the afternoon, which is right in the middle of rush hour.
At that time, almost everyone seems to be heading home.
The journey home usually takes around fifty minutes, so it's slightly longer than my morning journey.
I think the main reason is that traffic in the afternoon is more spread out across different roads, and people are generally more tired after a full day at work.
Personally, I find the journey home quite mentally tiring.
After spending eight hours at work, the last thing I really want to do is sit in traffic.
Sometimes, I just want to get home as quickly as possible, have a shower and relax.
However, there is one small positive thing about riding a motorbike.
I have a certain amount of freedom.
I don't have to follow a fixed route, and I can sometimes choose a smaller road if the main road is too crowded.
I can also stop somewhere to buy food or drinks on the way home.
That's one of the main reasons why I still prefer using a motorbike.
It's not necessarily the most comfortable form of transportation, but it's flexible.
In Ho Chi Minh City, flexibility is actually quite important because traffic conditions can change very quickly.
Having said that, I don't think using a motorbike is the perfect long-term solution for the city.
There are simply too many private vehicles on the roads.
I sometimes think about using public transportation in the future.
Buses are one possible option.
I have used buses before, and the price is definitely attractive.
However, the main problem is that buses are also affected by traffic jams.
If the roads are crowded, the bus can be very slow.
In addition, I would probably have to walk or take another form of transport to get from the bus stop to my office.
The metro is a more interesting option for me.
If the metro network in Ho Chi Minh City becomes more developed in the future and there is a convenient route from my area to somewhere near District 1, I would seriously consider using it.
I think the metro could be much more predictable than travelling by motorbike.
You don't have to worry as much about traffic jams, and you can probably use the journey to relax or listen to something.
For example, I could listen to an English podcast on the way to work.
Of course, there would still be some practical problems.
I would need to travel from my apartment to the nearest station, and then from the station to my office.
So, the whole journey might require a combination of different forms of transportation.
But honestly, I think that's probably the future of transportation in a large city like Ho Chi Minh City.
People may not use just one form of transport.
They might ride a motorbike to a metro station, take the metro and then walk to work.
Overall, my daily commute is not particularly exciting, but it is a very important part of my everyday life.
I spend around an hour and a half commuting every day, which adds up to quite a lot of time over a year.
Sometimes, I complain about the traffic, the heat and the rain.
But at the same time, commuting has become such a normal part of my routine that I can't really imagine my daily life without it.
So, if I had to describe my daily commute in Ho Chi Minh City, I would say it's tiring, unpredictable and sometimes frustrating, but it's also a very typical experience of living and working in a busy city.
And personally, I really hope that public transportation, especially the metro, will become more convenient in the future, because I wouldn't mind having a less stressful way to get to work every day.
